Module 2

The Rise of Artificial Intelligence

From Turing machines to ChatGPT — 70+ years of AI progress through breakthroughs, hype cycles, and two AI winters.

1950s 1960–70s AI Winter I 1980s AI Winter II 1990s 2000s 2010s 2020s

Foundations

1950s
1950

Alan Turing publishes Computing Machinery and Intelligence and proposes the Turing Test.

1952

Arthur Samuel develops one of the first self-learning checkers programs at IBM.

1956

Dartmouth Conference: John McCarthy coins the term Artificial Intelligence.

1958

McCarthy creates LISP, the primary AI programming language for decades.

1959

Rosenblatt's perceptron demonstrates early neural network classification.

Rule-Based Systems

1960s–1970s

Key Programs

  • ELIZA (1966): early chatbot that mimicked a therapist.
  • SHRDLU (1970): natural-language understanding in a blocks world.

Challenges

  • Worked mainly in small and constrained environments.
  • Early machine translation efforts struggled.
  • Computers were expensive and limited.

Reality

  • Most AI success remained inside research labs.
  • Real-world complexity was much harder than expected.

The First AI Winter

1974–1980

Why It Happened

  • AI promises were too optimistic for available hardware.
  • Computers lacked enough power and memory.
  • Systems only handled narrow experiments.

What Happened

  • Government funding was cut in the US and UK.
  • Many AI labs and projects shut down.
  • Researchers moved to other fields.
What Survived
Small groups kept neural network ideas alive. Progress slowed, but it never fully stopped.

Expert Systems Boom

1980s
$1 Billion

Annual spend on AI hardware (Lisp machines) by 1985.

500+

Expert systems deployed in US companies by 1986.

2/3

Fortune 500 companies using expert systems by 1988.

The Second AI Winter

1987–1993

1. Market Collapse

Lisp machines were outperformed by cheaper general-purpose workstations.

2. Maintenance Crisis

Maintaining rule-based systems became costlier than their value.

3–5. Failed Delivery + Funding Cuts

Japan's 5th generation goals stalled, DARPA reduced support, and companies exited AI.

Seeds of the Future
Backpropagation, statistical methods, and Bayesian approaches survived and became the core of modern AI.

Machine Learning Begins

1990s
1997

Deep Blue defeats world chess champion Garry Kasparov.

1995–1999

Support Vector Machines and related methods improve classification and detection tasks.

1998

Neural networks begin practical handwritten ZIP code recognition.

Late 1990s

The internet generates web-scale data that becomes fuel for the next AI wave.

Big Data + Better Algorithms

2000s

Data

  • Digitization of books, records, and media.
  • Social network behavior data at massive scale.
  • ImageNet and other large labeled datasets.

Computing Power

  • Multi-core CPUs broadened practical compute.
  • Cloud compute became accessible (AWS launch, 2006).

Real-World Applications

  • Search and ranking improvements.
  • Email spam filtering.
  • Recommendation systems (e.g., media and commerce).

Deep Learning Revolution

2010s

2012 — AlexNet

GPU-trained deep CNNs dramatically reduce ImageNet error rates.

2014 — GANs

Generative Adversarial Networks accelerate synthetic media research.

2016 — AlphaGo

Defeats Lee Sedol 4–1, showing stronger strategic pattern learning.

2018 — BERT & GPT-1

Transfer learning and pretraining transform modern NLP performance.

2019 — GPT-2

Large-scale language generation sparks mainstream awareness and safety discussions.

Generative AI Era

2020s

Foundation Models

  • GPT-3 showed major capability scaling effects.
  • AI can draft, summarize, translate, and generate code.

Generative Media

  • DALL·E, Stable Diffusion, and Midjourney enter production workflows.
  • Text-to-video and voice cloning broaden synthetic media use.

Agents & Reasoning

  • Prompting and tool use improve planning and reasoning behavior.
  • AI coding assistants become everyday tools for software teams.

Key Take-Aways

  1. Hype cycles are built into AI's DNA.
  2. Breakthroughs are seeds, not one-time events.
  3. Scale changes what becomes possible.
  4. Technology often advances faster than governance.
  5. AI is brilliant for many narrow tasks but not generally intelligent.
